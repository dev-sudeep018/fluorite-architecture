A small live experiment, run entirely in your browser

Does h learn patience, or just smooth things out?

Three tiny Hopfield/EqProp networks play the same two-armed bandit. Plain gets no extra signal at all. EMA gets a fixed-time-constant running average of recent outcomes. h gets an attention readout over a stored memory of past episode outcomes \u2014 a small, real implementation of the "third settling system" idea from a few messages back.

The bandit's best arm sometimes flips for good (a genuine change), and sometimes flips briefly then reverts (a probe) \u2014 statistically identical to a real change while it's happening, distinguishable only by how long it lasts. The question: does h learn to tell short probes from long ones better than a fixed-rate average does, and does that sense of "how long is too long" actually adapt when the environment's own probe lengths change between phase 1 and phase 2?

The actual test

Spurious-switch rate, by probe length, phase 1 vs phase 2

For each probe, did the agent's preferred arm flip and stay flipped for at least two episodes \u2014 a switch the bandit never actually earned, since probes always revert? If h is doing something real, its phase-2 curve should sit visibly lower (more resistant) than its phase-1 curve, at most lengths \u2014 the building blocks for that resistance literally weren't present until enough longer-running probes had occurred in phase 2 to teach it that patience. A fixed-rate EMA has no mechanism to do this at all.
